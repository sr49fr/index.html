The Economic Impact of a Protocol Infrastructure in Global Trade

The current digital trade infrastructure is fragmented and inefficient. Companies must connect to multiple platforms, repeat compliance procedures, and reconcile the same data across systems, leading to significant and unnecessary costs.1,2

At the core of the problem lies data redundancy. A substantial share of the information in a typical trade transaction is entered multiple times across different systems. Industry analyses suggest that a large proportion of trade data is duplicated across documents such as invoices, bills of lading, and customs declarations.3,4 Each manual step introduces delays and increases the risk of errors. Trade facilitation research consistently shows that processing delays reduce trade efficiency and increase costs by tying up working capital and slowing the movement of goods.5

Digitalization helps address part of this problem. Moving from paper to electronic documents reduces manual work and can significantly lower administrative costs. The adoption of frameworks such as the UNCITRAL Model Law on Electronic Transferable Records has been shown to reduce processing times substantially and improve operational efficiency.6,7

However, digitalization alone does not solve the underlying issue. Even when documents are digital, companies must still verify identities, check counterparties, and confirm the accuracy of data. As a result, duplication, delays, and manual intervention persist. The root cause is not the format of documents, but the lack of a shared trust layer.8

Today, identity and trust are managed separately by each institution. The same company is verified multiple times by different banks, platforms, and regulators. Corporate onboarding can cost several thousand dollars per company, while full banking onboarding may reach between USD 14,000 and 30,000 depending on regulatory requirements.9,10 When these processes are repeated across institutions, costs multiply quickly.

Industry research indicates that a significant share of compliance and onboarding work involves duplicated verification tasks.11 Given that global spending on AML and KYC compliance exceeds USD 200 billion annually, even partial reductions through shared trust infrastructure could result in substantial efficiency gains.

Additional costs arise from system integration. Global spending on supply chain and digital infrastructure systems is measured in tens of billions of dollars annually, with a significant portion allocated to integrating and maintaining connections between incompatible systems.12,13 Gartner estimates that integration work can account for 25 to 40 percent of total application costs in large enterprises.14

A protocol-based infrastructure offers a fundamentally different architectural approach. Rather than integrating separately with each platform, organizations connect once to a shared protocol layer that reuses aligned trade document semantics over time, without replacing existing connections. Systems continue to operate within their own architectures while exchanging trusted data through standardized messages. In this model, identity is no longer bound to a single platform but can function seamlessly across systems.

This approach mirrors the architecture of the internet. Protocols such as TCP/IP and HTTP enable independent systems to communicate globally without requiring uniform internal systems.15,16 In the same way, global trade requires a common framework for trusted data exchange.

Applying these principles to trade infrastructure could significantly reduce integration complexity, accelerate onboarding, and enable automated verification of trade data. Scenario-based estimates derived from documented cost pools suggest that reducing duplication in onboarding, compliance, and interoperability could generate tens of billions of dollars in annual efficiency gains.

In summary, today's trade infrastructure is unnecessarily costly and increasingly complex. Compliance and verification requirements place disproportionate burdens on smaller participants, effectively excluding many from global markets. Digitalization reduces costs, but it does not fundamentally change how trade operates. Only when identity and data become verifiable can trust be embedded into the system. This is what enables true end-to-end automation and unlocks the full potential of global trade.

The Hidden Infrastructure Problem in Digital Trade

Global trade exceeds 30 trillion USD annually, according to the World Trade Organization.17 Despite decades of investment in digitalization, international trade operations remain fragmented. Companies, banks, logistics providers, and regulators operate hundreds of specialized digital platforms, yet these systems often cannot communicate with each other seamlessly.

The result is a paradox. Trade has become digital, but it has not become integrated. Firms frequently must onboard repeatedly to multiple platforms, perform identical Know Your Customer procedures with several financial institutions, and reconcile inconsistent data across systems. Even highly digitized supply chains still rely on manual reconciliation between software platforms. This creates a situation where digitalization can increase complexity and cost, especially for small and medium sized enterprises.

Several studies document this fragmentation problem. Corporate onboarding and compliance procedures for financial institutions can cost between 10,000 and 50,000 USD per client in complex cases.18 At the same time, global spending on anti-money laundering and KYC compliance exceeds 200 billion USD annually.19,20 A significant share of this spending results from duplicated verification activities across institutions.

The Asian Development Bank estimates the global trade finance gap at approximately 2.5 trillion USD, with small and medium sized enterprises disproportionately affected because compliance and onboarding requirements are too costly relative to the size of their transactions.

These findings suggest that the core challenge in digital trade is not the absence of platforms. Rather, it is the lack of a shared infrastructure layer that allows systems to exchange trusted data and recognize counterparties across platforms. This also explains why many platforms struggle to achieve network effects and fail to scale.

From Digitalization to Automation: The Missing Trust Layer

Many people believe the main goal of digital trade is to eliminate paper and manual data entry. This is correct, but it is only part of the solution. Digitalization makes processes faster and cheaper. Documents can be exchanged electronically and some manual work disappears. However, even in fully digital processes with standardized exchange of machine-readable data, companies still need to check who they are dealing with, verify information, and ensure everything is correct. As a result, many processes are still not fully automated. People remain involved because trust is not built into the system.21

The real issue is not paper. It is trust. Today, trust is created through repeated checks, onboarding processes, and intermediaries such as banks or platforms. This leads to delays, costs, and duplication — structural friction that cannot be removed by digitizing documents alone.22,23

A different approach is to make trust part of the infrastructure. Using modern technology, identities and data can be made directly end-to-end verifiable. Instead of sending documents that need to be reviewed, systems exchange information that can be automatically checked for authenticity and accuracy.24,25

This changes how trade works. Systems no longer need to trust first and verify later. They can verify instantly. Only then does true end-to-end automation become possible. Processes can run across organizations without manual intervention, making trade faster, simpler, and more scalable. In short: digitalization improves efficiency. Adding a trust layer makes automation possible.

Why This Matters Now: Convergence Requires an Interoperability Layer

Global trade is entering a phase of accelerated convergence. Standards bodies have aligned core data elements across key trade documents,26 legal frameworks increasingly recognize digital equivalents of paper instruments, and platforms across finance, logistics, and compliance are rapidly digitizing their processes. Yet despite this progress, trade remains fragmented at the level where it matters most: how data actually moves between participants.

Today's systems are still largely connected through bespoke integrations, platform-specific formats, and duplicated data exchanges. This creates friction, increases costs, and limits scalability. Each new connection requires time, coordination, and technical effort, making it difficult to achieve true network effects across the ecosystem.

What is missing is a shared interoperability layer that operates "on the wire" between systems. Such a layer does not replace existing platforms or standards. Instead, it enables them to communicate in a consistent, verifiable, and secure way. It ensures that aligned data models can be exchanged seamlessly, that digital documents can be trusted across organizational boundaries, and that interactions between parties can be validated without relying on centralized intermediaries.

As legal recognition of digital trade documents advances and standards continue to converge, the absence of this interoperability layer becomes more visible. Without it, the full benefits of digital trade cannot be realized. With it, the ecosystem can move from isolated digital islands to a truly connected, scalable, and trustworthy global trade infrastructure.

In practice, this means that existing document networks, logistics utilities, Single Windows, and finance platforms can recognize and act on each other's trusted data and control events across borders without altering their internal architectures. The protocol layer builds on established standards such as UN/CEFACT data models, GS1 identifiers, and WCO frameworks, while aligning with legal concepts of control and integrity defined in MLETR and national implementations such as the Electronic Trade Documents Act 2023.

From Documents to Data and Events

Traditional trade processes evolved around documents such as purchase orders, bills of lading, warehouse receipts, and invoices. Digital systems often replicate this model by transmitting electronic documents instead of paper.27

From a data architecture perspective, however, a document is simply a container for many pieces of information captured at a particular moment in time. Trade transactions actually consist of connected data elements that evolve throughout the lifecycle of a transaction.28

For example, an invoice contains identities of trading partners, product information, quantities, payment terms, and pricing details. Much of this information is duplicated across other documents such as shipping notices, customs declarations, and financing records.29

Modern digital architecture therefore increasingly models complex systems as interconnected data structures rather than static documents. In such models, individual data elements can evolve independently while maintaining relationships with other elements.

Event-driven architecture has become a widely adopted principle in distributed systems. Instead of transmitting entire documents, systems exchange event messages that update the state of a transaction. Supply chain events may represent actions such as ordering goods, dispatching shipments, issuing invoices, or executing payments.30

ISTTP's role here is deliberately small but decisive: it standardizes the envelope and proofs — who asserted what, with what authority, and when — so existing systems can emit and consume trusted events without adopting a new platform.

Organizations such as the World Wide Web Consortium and the Open Group promote similar approaches through linked data models and event-driven system architectures. Within trade ecosystems, standards bodies such as UN/CEFACT advocate structured data models that represent trade information as interoperable data elements rather than static documents.

Identity and Identifiers as the Backbone of Automation

When trade transactions are represented as connected data elements and events, reliable identity becomes essential. Automated systems must be able to determine whether two references correspond to the same entity — whether an organization, a business location, a product, or a device. In traditional paper-based processes, identity is inferred from names, addresses, signatures, barcodes, and derived analytics. Machines cannot rely on such assumptions. Instead, each entity must have a persistent identifier that acts as a stable reference across systems.31

One widely adopted identifier for legal entities is the Legal Entity Identifier, administered by the Global Legal Entity Identifier Foundation. The LEI system was introduced after the global financial crisis to improve transparency in financial markets and now covers more than two million legal entities worldwide.32,33 Persistent identifiers enable digital systems to link events and data objects reliably. They also allow attributes associated with entities to evolve over time while preserving historical continuity and audit trails.

To enable automated verification of attributes, modern digital identity frameworks use verifiable credentials. These cryptographically secured statements allow trusted authorities to attest claims about organizations or individuals in a machine-verifiable way. Using such mechanisms, a company can prove that it holds a valid license or registration without sharing the entire document with each interaction.

These mechanisms form the basis of automated trust infrastructure, allowing machines to verify identities, roles, and permissions instantly across digital networks. In this sense, identity becomes a key building block for the future of digital trade.

Privacy, Confidentiality, and Selective Disclosure

Trade transactions contain highly sensitive commercial information, including pricing agreements, supplier relationships, logistics routes, and production volumes. Protecting this information is essential for maintaining competitiveness. Digitalization changes the balance between required transparency and business confidentiality. A key challenge in digital trade systems is therefore balancing interoperability with data protection.34

Modern digital identity frameworks address this challenge through selective disclosure mechanisms. Selective disclosure allows systems to reveal only the specific attributes required for a particular interaction. For example, a logistics provider may receive shipment details without seeing pricing information, while a financing bank may verify payment terms without accessing broader supply chain data.35

In addition to protecting data content, digital trade systems must also protect metadata. Metadata describes communication patterns such as which parties interact and how frequently. Even when message content is encrypted, metadata can reveal sensitive business relationships. Research shows that metadata analysis can expose behavioral patterns and business connections.36

Architectures based on peer-to-peer data exchange can reduce these risks by avoiding centralized data repositories. Instead of broadcasting information to centralized platforms, participants exchange data directly when required for a specific business interaction. This reduces unnecessary data exposure and limits systemic risk.

Regulatory frameworks such as the European Union's General Data Protection Regulation reinforce the importance of privacy by design in digital systems. These frameworks require that data protection is built into system architecture from the outset, rather than added as an afterthought.
